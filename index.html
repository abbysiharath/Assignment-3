    Group Processes


    Effective communication was a big part of how successfully the group worked together. Each participant was attentive and made an attempt to show up to meetings. These sessions were scheduled regularly (two per week was the goal), and when they weren't, it was rather simple to reschedule because other commitments like other classes, work schedules, or personal obligations were acknowledged.

    Members were also kept informed whether they had missed all or part of a meeting by using the meeting notes. Even while participants were often working on their own projects during meetings, they were often productive. Meetings, for instance, were supposed to last only a short while, but they frequently did, demonstrating the group's dedication.

    There were some meetings where nobody showed up for a variety of reasons. Nevertheless, regardless of meetings, each participant gave the assignment their best effort. The group meetings should be scheduled more efficiently; finding an extension that allows members’ input for availabilities.

    The group as a whole agrees that the development was a little slow. They said it would be perfect if some of the assignments were finished and turned in a little earlier than they were. Additionally, getting started on the project idea earlier will be an excellent idea. In Assignment 2, with the teacher's assistance, the group chose to tackle a fresh idea, but this aspect of the assignment wasn't really addressed until the final few days before submission. Hopefully in this final assignment the project idea won’t be dismissed until the final week, and instead it can be worked on gradually with a foolproof process plan.

    Career Plans


    The professional paths of our group are rather similar. Even though each candidate is vying for a different position, they all have complementary skills and traits. As a group, we realised that fundamental skills like knowing how to use Microsoft and programming are interchangeable in the IT industry.

    When comparing the talents and prerequisites for our dream occupations, it becomes clear that soft skills like communication, time management, and the capacity for teamwork are frequently valued over hard skills. In terms of hard skills, we all need to have prior knowledge of Microsoft (excel, windows, etc.). Before applying for a full-time career, more specialised occupations increasingly require knowledge of java, html, completion of credentials (CCNA), and of course years of experience.

    The group would be well-suited to activities like system management, data management, and programming. You need to be proficient in coding languages like HTML, JavaScript, and CSS to be a front-end developer. This can be different for a digital strategist's job, which necessitates a deeper knowledge of marketing. Despite the fact that they are both IT-related, they are completely different professions with extremely different duties and demands.

    After analysing the burning glass data from Assignment 2, along with feedback from Assignment 1&2 and further research, some of our group members’ perspectives on their ideal jobs has slightly changed. Although they do not yet have a clear cut vision on the position they’d want to pursue, the factors mentioned above have encouraged them to keep an open mind and consider all alternatives.

    This is generally due to the discovery that skills learned and experience obtained on the journey to becoming an IT professional can be transferrable across a majority of jobs in the industry. Skills like writing, troubleshooting, and planning may not be prerequisites like knowledge in a coding language, but would make our daily lives a lot more efficient, increase employability and make workloads less demanding.

    In saying this, some group members are also encouraged to follow a straight road and achieve the one position they’ve set their eyes on. Strong demand for careers in the IT sector helps with this, considering that IT has and will continue to have a great impact on the world. Nevertheless, there is the security that in the event of a career change, skills that have been learned will be transferrable to other jobs both in and outside the IT industry.
